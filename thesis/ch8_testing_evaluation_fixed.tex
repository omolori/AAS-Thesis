\chapter{Testing and Evaluation}
\label{chap:testing}

This chapter presents the quantitative evaluation of the three execution pipelines implemented in
Chapter~\ref{chap:implementation}.
The central question is whether injecting simulation parameters from the Asset Administration Shell
(AAS) causes the virtual robot to match the physical UR3 more closely than a default, unconfigured
simulation.
To answer this, joint position accuracy, end-effector path deviation, and motor current are compared
across all three pipelines, with the physical robot as the ground-truth reference.

The chapter is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:eval_methodology} describes the evaluation approach.
Section~\ref{sec:eval_dataset} summarises the collected data.
Section~\ref{sec:timing_analysis} analyses execution timing.
Sections~\ref{sec:joint_accuracy} to~\ref{sec:current_analysis} present the accuracy results by
metric, and Section~\ref{sec:eval_summary} synthesises all findings.


%% ===================================================================
\section{Evaluation Methodology}
\label{sec:eval_methodology}
%% ===================================================================

The evaluation follows a quasi-experimental design
\cite{cook1979quasi, wieringa2014design} in which the physical UR3 robot provides reference
measurements and the two simulation variants act as treatment conditions.
The three pipelines, introduced in Section~\ref{sec:implementation_overview} and illustrated in
Figure~\ref{fig:digital_twin_overview}, are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Pipeline 1 (real)}: the physical UR3 robot executing the pick-and-place
          trajectory in the AAU laboratory, recording data via the RTDE interface at 125\,Hz;

    \item \textbf{Pipeline 2 (sim\_no\_aas)}: the URSim virtual robot running with default
          factory parameters and no AAS connection;

    \item \textbf{Pipeline 3 (sim\_aas)}: the URSim virtual robot with payload mass and
          tool-centre-point (TCP) offset injected live from the BaSyx AAS server immediately
          before execution (as described in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline3_sequence}).
\end{itemize}

All three pipelines execute the same pick-and-place trajectory and record the same set of RTDE
signals: joint positions, joint velocities, TCP pose, TCP speed, joint current, and controller
runtime state.
Data are stored in a shared Supabase (PostgreSQL) database, so every comparison can always be
reproduced from the raw records.
Pairwise comparisons (Pipeline~1 versus Pipeline~2, and Pipeline~1 versus Pipeline~3) are
performed by the \texttt{comparator.py} module, which aligns the two runs via linear interpolation
onto the shorter timeline before computing per-joint RMSE, TCP RMS deviation, and RMS joint
current.

The evaluation addresses three concrete questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Does Pipeline~3 (\textit{sim\_aas}) match the real robot's joint trajectory more
          closely than Pipeline~2 (\textit{sim\_no\_aas})?
    \item Does Pipeline~3 reproduce the real robot's TCP path more closely?
    \item Does Pipeline~3 reproduce the real robot's motor current more closely?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Execution Timing Analysis}
\label{sec:timing_analysis}
%% ===================================================================

Execution time is the first indicator of how closely each pipeline reproduces the physical robot's
behaviour.
Figure~\ref{fig:cycle_time} compares the mean per-cycle duration across all evaluation runs, and
Table~\ref{tab:timing} provides the full breakdown including sample count and cycle-time
statistics.

    \caption{Mean per-cycle execution time for the three pipelines.
             Error bars show the standard deviation across evaluation runs.
             Both simulation pipelines are consistently slower than the physical robot due to
             RTDE command-processing overhead in URSim.}
    \label{fig:cycle_time}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Timing breakdown for the three representative evaluation runs.
         Cycle times for \textit{sim\_aas} were read from the monitoring dashboard
         because an approximately 19.5\,s initialisation overhead at run start shifts the first
         home-position return out of detection range.}
\label{tab:timing}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Pipeline & Duration (s) & Samples & Cycles & Mean cycle (s) \\
\midrule
\textit{real}        & 110.01 & 13{,}752 & 3 & $34.67 \pm 0.02$ \\
\textit{sim\_no\_aas} & 125.17 & 15{,}647 & 3 & $39.66 \pm 0.08$ \\
\textit{sim\_aas}     & 139.07 & 17{,}385 & 3 & $\approx 39.8 \pm 0.04$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Two timing effects are visible in the results.

\textbf{Both simulation pipelines run slower per cycle than the physical robot.}
Each pick-and-place cycle takes approximately 39.7\,s in the simulator versus 34.7\,s on the
real robot, a difference of roughly 5\,s.
The physical robot benefits from a dedicated real-time OS kernel with hardware-level motion
interpolation.
URSim processes RTDE commands in a software loop, which introduces consistent latency on every
\texttt{moveJ} instruction regardless of whether AAS parameters are applied.
This gap is intrinsic to the simulator architecture and independent of the AAS layer.

\textbf{Pipeline 3 (\textit{sim\_aas}) starts approximately 19.5\,s later than Pipeline 2.}
This overhead arises from the AAS parameter fetch, controller setup, and RTDE reconnection that
happen before the first trajectory command, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline3_sequence}.
Because the accuracy comparator aligns runs by wall-clock time rather than cycle index, this
offset shifts the \textit{sim\_aas} trajectory forward in time relative to \textit{real}.
The comparator then matches trajectory segments from different points in the cycle, which
directly inflates every per-joint RMSE value reported in the next section.
This is an important caveat when interpreting the position accuracy results.

\textbf{Joints 3 and 4 dominate the error.}
J3 and J4 account for roughly 75\,\% of the total RMSE in both comparisons.
These are the elbow and wrist-1 joints, which execute the largest angular excursions during the
pick-and-place motion.
Small differences in the simulation's acceleration profile and RTDE command timing accumulate
most noticeably at high-travel joints.

\textbf{Pipeline 3 (\textit{sim\_aas}) shows higher RMSE than Pipeline 2 at every joint.}
This is the opposite of the improvement that AAS-driven calibration would ideally produce, and
it requires careful explanation.
Two factors contribute.
First, the UR3 controller operates in \emph{joint space}: \texttt{moveJ} commands specify target
joint angles directly, and the controller's internal dynamics model compensates for any attached
payload.
Injecting the payload mass updates the torque compensation, but the commanded joint-angle
profile is identical regardless of whether a payload is registered.
Kinematic position accuracy is therefore structurally unaffected by payload injection.
Second, the 19.5\,s initialisation overhead means that \textit{sim\_aas} and \textit{real} are
never in the same phase of the trajectory when they are compared, inflating every per-joint RMSE
value.

The null result on kinematic accuracy is therefore not evidence that AAS has no value; it is
evidence that joint-space position tracking is not the correct metric to target when the only
injectable parameters are payload mass and TCP offset.
Section~\ref{sec:interface_constraints} explains why those are the only parameters available to
this pipeline.

temporal misalignment rather than a structural difference in path following.

\section{Simulation Interface Constraints}
\label{sec:interface_constraints}
%% ===================================================================

A fundamental factor limiting what the AAS can improve in this setup is the narrow parameter
API exposed by URSim.
As noted in the delimitations (Section~\ref{sec:delimitations}), the URSim scripting interface
accepts only two robot-configuration calls:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{set\_payload(mass, cog)}: registers the payload mass in kilograms and its
          centre-of-gravity offset in the tool frame;
    \item \texttt{set\_tcp(pose)}: sets the tool-centre-point transformation.
\end{itemize}

The AAS model developed for this project defines four submodels:
\textit{MotionCommand}, \textit{DynamicsParameters}, \textit{RobotState}, and
\textit{PerformanceKPIs}.
The \textit{DynamicsParameters} submodel contains four properties that are physically meaningful
for simulation fidelity: friction coefficients, a current noise level, a control latency, and a
damping factor.
None of these has a corresponding URSim API call; they are silently ignored by the injection
pipeline.

This constraint has two measurable consequences that are directly visible in the results.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Near-zero current at low-motion joints.}
          URSim has no friction or back-EMF model, so joints J1, J5, and J6 show near-zero
          simulated current even though the physical robot draws meaningful current at the same
          joints during low-velocity phases (see Section~\ref{sec:current_analysis}).
          No friction parameter value, however accurately calibrated, can correct this without a
          corresponding simulator API.

    \item \textbf{No kinematic improvement from payload injection.}
          The \texttt{moveJ} joint-space control architecture means that payload mass affects only
          the internal torque model, not the commanded position profile.
          A kinematic RMSE improvement through payload injection alone is structurally
          impossible.
\end{enumerate}

These are not failures of the AAS concept; they are boundary conditions imposed by the target
simulator.
A physics-based simulator that exposes mass, inertia, friction, and damping parameters through a
programmatic API would allow the full \textit{DynamicsParameters} submodel to be consumed,
potentially yielding measurable improvements in both kinematic accuracy and current fidelity.
This point is addressed in the Discussion (Chapter~\ref{chap:discussion}).

\section{Joint Current Analysis}
\label{sec:current_analysis}
%% ===================================================================

Motor current provides a complementary view of simulation fidelity that is sensitive to the
dynamic model rather than just the kinematic trajectory.
Figure~\ref{fig:rms_current} shows the RMS current drawn at each joint across all three
pipelines, and Table~\ref{tab:current} gives the exact values.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.90\textwidth]{../thesis_figures/fig8_rms_current_bar.pdf}
    \caption{RMS motor current per joint for all three pipelines.
             \textit{sim\_aas} closely matches the real robot at J2 and J3, confirming that
             AAS payload injection improved current fidelity at the high-load shoulder and
             elbow joints.
             Both simulation pipelines show near-zero current at J1, J5, and J6 because URSim
             has no friction model.}
    \label{fig:rms_current}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{RMS motor current (A) per joint for the three evaluation runs.
         The mean row averages over the six joints.}
\label{tab:current}
\begin{tabular}{lrrr}
\toprule
Joint           & \textit{real} & \textit{sim\_no\_aas} & \textit{sim\_aas} \\
\midrule
J1 -- Base      & 0.2767 & 0.0035 & 0.0036 \\
J2 -- Shoulder  & 1.2681 & 1.0906 & 1.2558 \\
J3 -- Elbow     & 1.1662 & 1.0001 & 1.2028 \\
J4 -- Wrist 1   & 0.6691 & 0.4321 & 0.5688 \\
J5 -- Wrist 2   & 0.1984 & 0.0100 & 0.0109 \\

\midrule

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Two distinct patterns emerge from Table~\ref{tab:current}.

\textbf{J1, J5, and J6 are near zero in both simulation pipelines.}
As explained in Section~\ref{sec:interface_constraints}, URSim has no friction or back-EMF
model.
The physical robot draws 0.28\,A, 0.20\,A, and 0.23\,A respectively at these joints even during
low-velocity phases, primarily from static holding torque and electromagnetic drag.
Because the simulator cannot model these effects, no AAS parameter can correct them.

\textbf{AAS injection substantially improved J2 and J3 current fidelity.}
This is the primary positive finding of the experiment.

\begin{itemize}
    \item J2 (Shoulder): \textit{sim\_no\_aas} drew 1.091\,A, which is 86\,\% of the real
          value of 1.268\,A.
          \textit{sim\_aas} drew 1.256\,A, raising the match to 99\,\% of real.

    \item J3 (Elbow): \textit{sim\_no\_aas} drew 1.000\,A, which is 86\,\% of the real
          value of 1.166\,A.
          \textit{sim\_aas} drew 1.203\,A, raising the match to 103\,\% of real.
\end{itemize}

The improvement is explained by the physics of the robot.
The shoulder and elbow joints support the entire arm against gravity, so their current draw is
proportional to the total suspended mass.
When the payload mass is set to the correct value of 1.2\,kg, the simulator allocates
proportionally larger compensation torques to J2 and J3, bringing their simulated current draw
much closer to physical measurements.

The overall mean current increased from 0.423\,A (\textit{sim\_no\_aas}) to 0.507\,A
(\textit{sim\_aas}), narrowing the gap to the real mean of 0.634\,A.
The remaining deficit is almost entirely attributable to the near-zero J1, J5, and J6 values,
which require friction modelling to resolve.

\section{Summary of Findings}
\label{sec:eval_summary}
%% ===================================================================

\midrule
Mean RMS current (A)            & 0.423      & 0.507              & $\uparrow$ $+$0.084\,A \\

J3 current (\% of real)         & 86\,\%     & 103\,\%            & $\uparrow$ $+$17\,pp \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The experiment yields a \textbf{split outcome} that must be read carefully.

On \textbf{kinematic position accuracy}, Pipeline~3 (\textit{sim\_aas}) produced no improvement
over Pipeline~2.
The higher RMSE figures are an artefact of the 19.5\,s initialisation overhead, which shifts the
two runs out of phase before any trajectory data are compared.
Even without this timing effect, the joint-space control architecture means that payload mass
cannot alter the commanded position profile; a kinematic RMSE improvement through payload
injection alone is structurally impossible.

On \textbf{current fidelity}, Pipeline~3 produced a clear and measurable improvement.
J2 rose from 86\,\% to 99\,\% of real, and J3 rose from 86\,\% to 103\,\% of real.
This finding directly confirms the AAS hypothesis at the dynamic level: when the simulator
receives accurate payload information, it distributes compensation torques correctly across the
shoulder and elbow joints, producing current signals that closely match the physical
measurements.

The persistent near-zero current at J1, J5, and J6 in both simulation pipelines identifies
friction and drag modelling as the principal remaining fidelity gap, one that requires a richer
simulator parameter interface to close regardless of AAS data quality.
